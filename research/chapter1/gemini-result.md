# Chapter I: General framework of EU-China relations


## 1.1. Evolution of the EU-China strategic partnership, 1975-2025


### 1. Short research summary

The bilateral relationship between the European Union and the People's Republic of China has undergone a profound structural transformation over the past five decades, evolving from nascent diplomatic engagement during the Cold War to a highly complex, multidimensional landscape characterized simultaneously by deep economic interdependence and acute geopolitical rivalry. The formal establishment of diplomatic relations on May 6, 1975, marked the genesis of this partnership, initiated by the visit of Sir Christopher Soames, then Vice-President of the European Economic Commission, to Beijing, where he successfully negotiated the terms of engagement with Chinese Premier Zhou Enlai (European External Action Service, "Conference on 50 Years of EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>1</sup> During this early phase, China viewed the project of European integration through a highly favorable strategic lens, perceiving a united Europe as an essential stabilizing factor for world peace and a necessary counterbalance to the bipolar hegemony of the era (European External Action Service, "Conference on 50 Years of EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>1</sup> Following the appointment of the first Chinese ambassador to the European Community, Li Lien-Pi, in September 1975, the foundational architecture of the relationship was codified through the signing of the first bilateral trade agreement in 1978 and the subsequent, more comprehensive Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement in 1985 (European External Action Service, "Conference on 50 Years of EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>1</sup>

This trajectory of optimistic engagement suffered a severe political rupture following the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests, which resulted in the immediate imposition of an EU arms embargo that remains fully enforced to the present day (Wikipedia, "China–European Union relations", 2025).<sup>2</sup> Despite this enduring security restriction, the 1990s and early 2000s witnessed a rapid normalization and expansion of commercial ties, inextricably linked to China's rapid integration into the global economic architecture and its pivotal accession to the World Trade Organization in 2001. This era of commercial primacy culminated in the formal establishment of the "Comprehensive Strategic Partnership" in 2003, a designation that reflected mutual aspirations for a multipolar world order and inaugurated a decade and a half of unprecedented market expansion, technological exchange, and the deep integration of global supply chains (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China, "China and the EU", 2025).<sup>3</sup>

However, as the balance of global economic power shifted, the European Union's strategic calculus underwent a definitive paradigm shift. In March 2019, the European Commission published the landmark "EU-China – A strategic outlook," a joint communication that permanently altered the diplomatic lexicon by formally categorizing China simultaneously across four distinct policy dimensions: a cooperation partner, a negotiating partner, an economic competitor, and a systemic rival promoting alternative models of governance (European Commission, "EU-China – A strategic outlook", 2019).<sup>4</sup> This multifaceted designation marked the definitive end of the era of uncritical engagement. Between 2020 and 2023, bilateral relations deteriorated further due to intensifying political frictions, most notably the freezing of the painstakingly negotiated Comprehensive Agreement on Investment following an exchange of mutual sanctions over human rights concerns in Xinjiang, as well as profound geopolitical divergence regarding China's tacit support for the Russian invasion of Ukraine (Bruegel, "Updating EU strategy on China", 2024).<sup>5</sup> As the relationship commemorates its 50th anniversary in May 2025, the European Union has pivoted sharply toward a doctrine of "economic security" and "de-risking," a posture designed to surgically mitigate vulnerabilities in critical supply chains and protect European technological sovereignty without pursuing a complete, economically catastrophic decoupling of the two global powers (European Commission, "Joint Communication on European Economic Security Strategy", 2023).<sup>7</sup>


### 2. Key verified facts

The historical evolution of the EU-China relationship is anchored by several critical, verifiable milestones that document the shift from diplomatic recognition to contemporary systemic rivalry. The formal commencement of diplomatic ties between the European Economic Community and the People's Republic of China occurred on May 6, 1975, a date solidified by the diplomatic mission of EEC Vice-President Sir Christopher Soames to Beijing and his subsequent agreement with Chinese Premier Zhou Enlai (European External Action Service, "Conference on 50 Years of EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>1</sup> The nascent relationship was quickly institutionalized; shortly after the initial visit, the Chinese government appointed its first ambassador to the European Community, Li Lien-Pi, who officially presented his credentials to the President of the Council of Ministers and the President of the Commission on September 15, 1975 (European External Action Service, "Conference on 50 Years of EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>1</sup> The economic dimension of the partnership was formally codified three years later with the signing of the first bilateral trade agreement in 1978, which was subsequently superseded and expanded by the landmark Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement signed on September 16, 1985 (EUR-Lex, "Council Regulation (EEC) No 2616/85", 1985).<sup>10</sup> This 1985 treaty legally mandated the creation of the EEC-China Joint Committee, establishing a permanent institutional mechanism to monitor bilateral cooperation (EUR-Lex, "Agreement on Trade and Economic Cooperation", 1985).<sup>1</sup>

Following the political disruptions of 1989 and the subsequent EU arms embargo, the relationship entered a period of rapid commercial acceleration, culminating in the official upgrading of bilateral ties to a "Comprehensive Strategic Partnership" in 2003, a designation intended to signal a mature, multi-tiered relationship covering trade, investment, and global governance (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China, "China and the EU", 2025).<sup>3</sup> However, the most consequential policy reassessment occurred on March 12, 2019, when the European Commission published the "EU-China – A strategic outlook" joint communication. This foundational text formally abandoned the monolithic view of China, explicitly designating the nation as a cooperation partner for global challenges, a negotiating partner for the balance of interests, an economic competitor in the pursuit of technological leadership, and a systemic rival promoting alternative governance models (European Commission, "EU-China – A strategic outlook", 2019).<sup>4</sup> To operationalize this new perspective, the 2019 Strategic Outlook outlined ten concrete action points, which included demanding that China peak its carbon emissions before 2030, calling for the rapid adoption of the International Procurement Instrument to ensure market reciprocity, and mandating the creation of a common European approach to secure 5G networks against foreign interference (European Commission, "EU-China – A strategic outlook", 2019).<sup>4</sup>

The practical application of this more assertive stance became evident between 2021 and 2025 with the suspension of the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment. Although an agreement in principle was reached in late 2020 after seven years of arduous negotiations, the ratification process was indefinitely frozen by the European Parliament in 2021 following the imposition of Chinese counter-sanctions against European lawmakers, human rights committees, and academic institutions (Institut Jacques Delors, "The EU-China relationship in 15 key dates", 2025).<sup>13</sup> Despite these profound structural tensions, high-level diplomatic engagement has continued into the 50th-anniversary year. In July 2025, the 25th China-EU Summit convened in Beijing, co-chaired by Chinese Premier Li Qiang alongside European Council President António Costa and European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, while the 13th round of the China-EU High-Level Strategic Dialogue took place contemporaneously in Brussels (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China, "China and the EU", 2025).<sup>3</sup>

### 4. Main academic interpretations

The academic literature analyzing the evolution of EU-China relations documents a distinct, progressive shift from liberal institutionalism to defensive realism. Scholars widely regard the 2019 "Systemic Rival" designation as a permanent watershed moment in European foreign policy strategy. According to analysts at the Brookings Institution, the formal introduction of the "systemic rivalry" terminology signaled Europe's institutional acknowledgment that decades of deep economic engagement would not inevitably induce Beijing to embrace liberal democratic values or converge with Western market principles (Brookings Institution, "How should Europe position itself", 2024).<sup>17</sup> This realization dismantled the foundational logic of the 2003 Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, fundamentally altering the bilateral dynamic from one based on assumed eventual convergence to one based on the management of permanent structural friction.

Furthermore, leading European think tanks such as MERICS and Bruegel highlight that while the political cohesion generated by the 2019 Strategic Outlook was a necessary corrective, the framework itself proved insufficient to manage the rapid deterioration of the global landscape. By 2024 and 2025, expert consensus suggested that the term "systemic rivalry" had become somewhat outdated and inadequate to describe the depth of the challenge, as China morphed from a conventional foreign policy and trade concern into a direct structural challenge to the internal prosperity, industrial base, and societal stability of European member states (Brookings Institution, "How should Europe position itself", 2024).<sup>17</sup> The current academic consensus emphasizes a new paradigm defined as "co-existence while de-risking." Scholars posit that bilateral relations will never return to their pre-COVID state of unfettered engagement because the inherent costs and geopolitical risks of asymmetrical dependence on China—particularly regarding critical inputs for Europe's decarbonization and digitalization agendas—now clearly outweigh the raw market benefits of unmitigated trade (Bruegel, "Updating EU strategy on China", 2024).<sup>5</sup>


### 5. Policy relevance for the thesis

This historical and evolutionary overview is absolutely critical for the foundation of an undergraduate thesis because it establishes the indispensable trajectory of EU-China relations, definitively demonstrating that the current economic frictions and trade disputes are not sudden, isolated anomalies, but rather the cumulative result of a long-term structural divergence. By critically examining the initial 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement, the thesis can illustrate the originally asymmetrical but highly cooperative nature of the relationship, which was fundamentally designed to integrate a developing China into the established global market architecture. Tracing the transition from the optimistic 2003 Comprehensive Strategic Partnership to the sobering 2019 Strategic Outlook provides the essential theoretical and policy framework required to explain why the European Union systematically transitioned from negotiating bilateral market access treaties to unilaterally constructing and deploying defensive trade mechanisms. This historical context seamlessly frames the thesis's subsequent, detailed exploration of contemporary economic security doctrines, legal frameworks, and the complex realities of supply chain de-risking.

1.2. Institutional framework and bilateral trade agreements


### 1. Short research summary

The institutional architecture governing the economic relationship between the European Union and China has undergone a profound metamorphosis, shifting from a foundational framework of cooperative bilateral dialogue mechanisms into a highly complex, increasingly adversarial legal regime defined by the aggressive deployment of defensive autonomous instruments. The legal baseline for this architecture was established by the 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement, which instituted a Joint Committee required to meet annually to monitor the implementation of cooperation schemes (EUR-Lex, "Agreement on Trade and Economic Cooperation", 1985).<sup>10</sup> As bilateral economic volumes expanded exponentially following China's 2001 accession to the World Trade Organization, the institutional framework adapted. Mechanisms such as the High-Level Economic and Trade Dialogue, established in 2008, became the primary diplomatic avenues for dispute resolution, strategic alignment, and the navigation of increasingly complex supply chains (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China, "China and the EU", 2025).<sup>3</sup>

A critical turning point in the trajectory of this legal framework was the arduous negotiation of the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment. After 35 exhaustive rounds of negotiations beginning in 2013, an agreement in principle was reached in December 2020, ostensibly designed to ensure level-playing-field rules, limit forced technology transfers, and guarantee reciprocal market access. However, the political environment rapidly deteriorated, and the ratification process was indefinitely suspended in 2021 following an exchange of sanctions. By 2025, EU trade officials explicitly confirmed there was "absolutely no intention" to revive the CAI due to persistent, systemic market barriers and the geopolitical realities of China's industrial overcapacity (European Commission, "EU-China Agreement on Investment", 2021; China Briefing, "EU-China Relations", 2025).<sup>18</sup> Conversely, a rare institutional success during this period was the 2020 Agreement on Geographical Indications, which entered into force in March 2021. This agreement initially protected 100 high-value agricultural GIs from both sides, automatically rejecting trademark misuse, with a phased expansion intended to protect 550 recognized GIs by the end of 2025 (European Union, "Agreement on Geographical Indications", 2020; EU SME Centre, "Geographical Indications", 2025).<sup>20</sup>

Faced with the paralysis of the WTO's dispute settlement appellate body and deeply asymmetrical market access conditions, the EU fundamentally restructured its institutional framework between 2023 and 2025 by deploying a formidable suite of unilateral trade defense instruments. In 2024 alone, the European Commission initiated a record 33 new trade defense investigations, targeting heavily subsidized Chinese imports in the chemical and steel sectors (European Commission, "Trade Defence Investigations", 2024).<sup>22</sup> Moving beyond traditional anti-dumping measures, the EU operationalized the Foreign Subsidies Regulation, launching unprecedented ex-officio investigations and dawn raids in 2024 and 2025 into massive Chinese state-owned entities such as CRRC, Nuctech, and the e-commerce platform Temu (White & Case, "Foreign Subsidies Regulation", 2025).<sup>24</sup> Furthermore, leveraging the new International Procurement Instrument, the EU enacted targeted legislation in 2025 explicitly restricting Chinese medical device companies from participating in EU public tenders exceeding €5 million, establishing a strict 50% limit on Chinese-origin inputs (European Commission, "IPI China Medical Devices", 2025).<sup>25</sup> Finally, the adoption of the Anti-Coercion Instrument (Regulation 2023/2675) signaled a definitive institutional shift from cooperative trade facilitation toward aggressive economic deterrence, empowering the Commission to swiftly retaliate against any third country attempting to interfere with the sovereign choices of EU member states through economic pressure (EUR-Lex, "Anti-Coercion Instrument", 2023).<sup>27</sup>


### 2. Key verified facts

The legal and institutional foundations of the EU-China economic relationship are defined by a series of specific, legally binding agreements and regulatory actions. The operational core of the early relationship was the Joint Committee Mechanism, legally mandated by the 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement. This committee was tasked with monitoring the functioning of the agreement and was required to meet once a year, alternating between Brussels and Beijing, thereby establishing the first permanent institutional bridge for economic diplomacy (EUR-Lex, "Agreement on Trade and Economic Cooperation", 1985).<sup>10</sup> Decades later, a significant legal milestone was achieved with the Geographical Indications Agreement, signed in September 2020 and active since March 2021. This treaty legally safeguards intellectual property by automatically rejecting trademark applications that misuse protected EU and Chinese GIs. The framework initially protected 100 GIs per side, expanded to roughly 200 by 2025, and established a strategic target at the 2025 EU-China summit to progressively increase this number to 550 recognized GIs (European Union, "Agreement on Geographical Indications", 2020; EU SME Centre, "Geographical Indications", 2025).<sup>20</sup>

In stark contrast to the success of the GI agreement, the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment represents a profound institutional failure. Although the EU and China successfully reached an agreement in principle in December 2020, the ratification process completely collapsed. This failure was primarily driven by the imposition of Chinese counter-sanctions against critical Members of the European Parliament, coupled with deep structural concerns regarding the lack of an enforceable dispute settlement mechanism for investment protection (European Commission, "EU-China Agreement on Investment", 2021; Institut Jacques Delors, "The EU-China relationship in 15 key dates", 2025).<sup>13</sup> As cooperative avenues closed, the EU pivoted to aggressive unilateral enforcement, resulting in a Trade Defence Activity Surge. In 2024, the European Commission launched 33 new trade defence investigations—the highest number recorded in any calendar year since 2006—with a specific, intense focus on Chinese imports in the chemical, clean technology, and steel sectors (European Commission, "Trade Defence Investigations", 2024; Ashurst, "Annual Report on EU Trade Defence", 2024).<sup>22</sup>

This surge in defensive action is deeply rooted in long-standing systemic disputes, most notably the Market Economy Status Dispute (WTO DS516). Initiated by China in December 2016, this WTO complaint challenged the EU's use of a "non-market economy" methodology in anti-dumping calculations (specifically contesting Articles 2.1 and 2.2 of the Anti-Dumping Agreement), underscoring the irreconcilable institutional friction regarding China's state-led, heavily subsidized economic model (World Trade Organization, "Dispute Settlement DS516", 2025).<sup>29</sup> To further combat these structural distortions, the EU enacted the Foreign Subsidies Regulation. During 2024 and 2025, the Commission utilized the FSR to launch highly intrusive ex-officio investigations—including unannounced dawn raids across multiple European member states—targeting Chinese state-owned rail equipment manufacturer CRRC, security scanner producer Nuctech, and the sprawling e-commerce provider Temu (White & Case, "Foreign Subsidies Regulation", 2025).<sup>24</sup>

To protect the integrity of its internal market and deter geopolitical blackmail, the EU adopted the Anti-Coercion Instrument in 2023. Regulation (EU) 2023/2675 provides the Commission with a rapid-response legal framework—featuring a standard four-month examination timeline—to apply severe countermeasures, such as punitive tariffs and procurement bans, if a third country attempts to unduly interfere with EU sovereign choices via economic pressure (EUR-Lex, "Anti-Coercion Instrument", 2023).<sup>27</sup> This deterrence strategy was further expanded into public procurement through the International Procurement Instrument. In June 2025, the EU formally adopted Implementing Regulation (EU) 2025/1197, a landmark measure that outright excludes Chinese corporate bids for medical equipment contracts exceeding €5 million and actively penalizes existing contracts that utilize more than 50% Chinese-origin inputs, a direct response to systemic discrimination against European medical device manufacturers within the Chinese domestic market (European Commission, "IPI China Medical Devices", 2025; Chambers and Partners, "Procurement of Medical Devices", 2025).<sup>25</sup>

### 4. Main academic interpretations

The radical transformation of the EU's legal framework regarding China has generated intense academic scrutiny, with the prevailing interpretation characterizing this evolution as a fundamental shift from "integration through law" to "security through law." The total collapse of the CAI ratification process and the subsequent, rapid proliferation of EU autonomous trade instruments are widely interpreted by legal scholars and political economists as a systemic, structural response to the inherent limitations of the paralyzed World Trade Organization. In-depth academic analyses of the Anti-Coercion Instrument argue compellingly that this specific regulation deliberately breaches the traditional, rigid boundaries between conventional commercial policy and high-stakes foreign and security policy. By legally granting the European Commission the unilateral authority to implement rapid-response tariffs and procurement blockades in response to geopolitical coercion, analysts at the European University Institute posit that the EU has finally weaponized its massive internal market to achieve broad strategic autonomy objectives that were previously reserved strictly for fragmented foreign policy frameworks (European University Institute, "EU Anti-Coercion Instrument", 2023).<sup>28</sup>

Conversely, Chinese academic perspectives and official government interpretations view these novel institutional mechanisms through a lens of intense hostility and suspicion. Following the European Commission's unprecedented application of the Foreign Subsidies Regulation through physical dawn raids on Chinese corporate offices in Europe, China's Ministry of Commerce initiated a formal, retaliatory trade and investment barrier investigation against the FSR itself. Chinese legal scholars and official MOFCOM statements assert that the FSR is a sophisticated tool of "de facto discrimination" deliberately engineered to selectively target highly competitive Chinese enterprises—particularly in critical green sectors like rail transport and photovoltaics—while intentionally sparing similarly situated international bidders from allied nations. From the Chinese academic viewpoint, these EU actions constitute a blatant violation of the principles of a level playing field and represent a descent into unvarnished protectionism masked as regulatory compliance (Cleary Gottlieb, "MOFCOM Issues Final Determination", 2025).<sup>34</sup>

An intimate understanding of these precise legal instruments is absolutely vital to substantiating the thesis's core argument: that the European Union has decisively abandoned its historical approach of seeking bilateral, negotiated compromises in favor of relying on muscular, unilateral regulatory enforcement to defend its internal market and project geoeconomic power.

1.3. Current challenges: de-risking vs. de-coupling


### 1. Short research summary

The contemporary, highly volatile phase of economic relations between the European Union and China is unequivocally defined by the overarching strategic doctrine of "de-risking." This fundamental policy shift was formally and publicly articulated by European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen during a watershed speech at the Mercator Institute for China Studies in March 2023, setting the stage for a dramatic recalibration of European industrial policy (European Commission, "Speech by President von der Leyen", 2023).<sup>35</sup> Within the official lexicon of the European Union, de-risking—which serves as the conceptual bedrock of the June 2023 European Economic Security Strategy—is meticulously defined not as a catastrophic severance of all economic ties (de-coupling), but rather as a highly targeted, surgical mitigation of strategic dependencies and severe supply-chain vulnerabilities, undertaken alongside the aggressive protection of the EU’s indigenous technological edge (European Commission, "Joint Communication on European Economic Security Strategy", 2023).<sup>7</sup> By contrast, full economic de-coupling is viewed by the highest echelons of EU leadership as a blunt, "black or white" response that is neither economically viable given the deep entwinement of global markets, nor ultimately in Europe's long-term strategic interest (Clingendael Institute, "National perspectives on Europe's de-risking", 2024).<sup>37</sup>

The practical, legislative implementation of this de-risking doctrine focuses overwhelmingly on three highly sensitive sectors: the acquisition of critical raw materials, the manufacturing of advanced electronics, and the deployment of clean energy technologies essential for the continent's climate transition. To directly counter the dangerous geographic concentration of global supply chains, the EU enacted the sweeping Critical Raw Materials (CRM) Act. This unprecedented legislation legally mandates that by the year 2030, no more than 65% of the European Union's annual consumption of any designated strategic raw material—such as the highly critical inputs of lithium, gallium, or rare earth elements—can be sourced from a single third country, an implicit but unmistakable reference to Chinese market dominance (European Commission, "European Critical Raw Materials Act", 2024).<sup>38</sup> Concurrently addressing the digital frontier, the Commission promulgated Recommendation (EU) 2023/2113, officially identifying four critical technology areas that demand immediate, exhaustive risk assessments by Member States to prevent catastrophic technology leakage: advanced semiconductors, artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and biotechnologies (European Commission, "Commission Recommendation on critical technology areas", 2023).<sup>40</sup>

The most highly visible and economically disruptive manifestation of this geoeconomic policy shift is the escalating commercial conflict over clean technologies. Confronted by vast, state-directed Chinese industrial subsidies and massive, resulting domestic overcapacity in critical sectors like solar photovoltaic panels and battery electric vehicles (EVs), the EU launched a series of high-profile, aggressively pursued anti-subsidy investigations. In October 2024, the Commission finalized its flagship investigation into battery electric vehicles, concluding that the Chinese value chain benefited from severe, unfair subsidies. Consequently, the EU imposed definitive countervailing duties ranging from 7.8% to 35.3% on Chinese electric vehicles, while simultaneously engaging in highly complex, ongoing negotiations over WTO-compatible "price undertakings" to establish minimum import prices (European Commission, "Commission issues Guidance Document", 2025).<sup>42</sup> Unsurprisingly, China rapidly retaliated by weaponizing its own trade defense instruments, initiating aggressive anti-dumping and anti-subsidy probes into lucrative European agricultural exports, culminating in the imposition of massive tariffs on European dairy, pork, and brandy (The Guardian, "China hits EU dairy industry with tariffs", 2025).<sup>44</sup>

Viewed from the corporate perspective operating on the ground, the business environment within China has deteriorated precipitously. The European Union Chamber of Commerce in China, in its comprehensive 2024/2025 Position Paper, explicitly warns that a sluggish post-pandemic economic recovery, profound and uncorrected industrial overcapacity, and a highly politicized, unpredictable market environment have severely dampened European business confidence. Outlining 1,043 specific recommendations for necessary reform, the Chamber's stark assessment emphasizes that the cascading risks of operating in the Chinese market are increasingly outweighing the historical commercial benefits, making further capital investments exceedingly difficult for corporate boards to justify (European Union Chamber of Commerce in China, "Position Paper 2024/2025", 2024).<sup>45</sup>


### 2. Key verified facts

The conceptual and practical execution of the de-risking strategy is anchored by specific policy definitions, legislative targets, and retaliatory actions. The foundational definition of De-risking was formally introduced by European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen as a comprehensive strategy deliberately designed to address excessive market dependencies through active supply chain diversification and enhanced domestic resilience, while explicitly and publicly rejecting the broader, more destructive concept of total economic de-coupling (European Commission, "Speech by President von der Leyen", 2023; Clingendael Institute, "National perspectives on Europe's de-risking", 2024).<sup>36</sup> This concept was codified into official policy through the European Economic Security Strategy, adopted in June 2023. This overarching strategy focuses intensely on three interdependent pillars: promoting the EU's internal competitiveness, ruthlessly protecting against economic security risks via newly minted tools like stringent FDI screening and the Anti-Coercion Instrument, and aggressively partnering with allied, like-minded nations (European Commission, "Joint Communication on European Economic Security Strategy", 2023).<sup>7</sup>

To operationalize this strategy and reduce reliance on China—which currently holds an overwhelming near-monopoly on critical elements like gallium and processed rare earths—the Critical Raw Materials (CRM) Act establishes legally binding, highly ambitious benchmarks for the year 2030. The Act legally mandates extracting at least 10%, processing a formidable 40%, and recycling a minimum of 25% of the EU’s annual strategic raw material needs domestically, coupled with a strict, non-negotiable limit stipulating that no more than 65% of any critical material can be imported from any single third country (European Commission, "European Critical Raw Materials Act", 2024).<sup>38</sup> Parallel to securing physical materials, the EU initiated a massive Critical Technology Risk Assessment. In October 2023, the Commission issued Recommendation (EU) 2023/2113, identifying a total of 10 critical technological areas while mandating that four specifically undergo urgent, comprehensive risk assessment with Member States: advanced semiconductors, artificial intelligence systems, quantum technologies, and advanced biotechnologies (European Commission, "Commission Recommendation on critical technology areas", 2023).<sup>40</sup>

The most profound real-world impact of this defensive posture occurred with the execution of the EV Anti-Subsidy Duties in 2024. Concluding an exhaustive, highly publicized investigation on October 29, 2024, the European Commission officially determined that the entire Chinese Battery Electric Vehicle (BEV) value chain benefits systematically from unfair, state-directed subsidies. Consequently, the EU imposed definitive, punitive countervailing duties ranging from a baseline of 7.8% up to a maximum of 35.3%, legally locked in for a period of five years (European Commission, "Commission issues Guidance Document", 2025).<sup>42</sup> Seeking a diplomatic off-ramp, by January 2025, China's Ministry of Commerce and the EU successfully negotiated a "Guidance Document on Submission of Price Undertaking Offers." This framework explores the implementation of strict minimum import prices as a viable, WTO-compliant alternative to the punitive EV duties (Ministry of Commerce of the People's Republic of China, "Notification on the Progress of China-EU Consultations", 2025).<sup>50</sup>

Despite these ongoing negotiations, the immediate economic fallout triggered massive Chinese Retaliatory Tariffs in 2025. In direct, transparent response to the EU's electric vehicle tariffs, Beijing weaponized its own trade defense arsenal, most notably imposing crushing provisional duties of up to 42.7% on vital EU dairy products in December 2025—specifically targeting high-value, geographically protected cheeses such as French Roquefort and Italian Gorgonzola—while running parallel, deeply intimidating probes into EU pork and brandy exports (The Guardian, "China hits EU dairy industry with tariffs", 2025).<sup>44</sup> This toxic macroeconomic environment has devastated European Business Confidence throughout 2024 and 2025. The EU Chamber of Commerce in China's highly anticipated 2024/2025 Position Paper reported historically low domestic economic growth and severe, systemic industrial overcapacity in key sectors like solar panels and electric vehicles. The Chamber grimly concluded that escalating political complexities, relentless domestic competition fueled by state aid, and unpredictable regulatory environments are forcing European multinational firms to fundamentally reconsider the viability of long-term capital investments within China's borders (European Union Chamber of Commerce in China, "Position Paper 2024/2025", 2024).<sup>45</sup>

### 4. Main academic interpretations

The intense intellectual debate contrasting de-risking against full de-coupling has generated a substantial, deeply polarized body of academic discourse. Leading European geopolitical analysts generally support the concept of de-risking as a pragmatic, inescapable necessity in the modern era. A comprehensive 2024 study commissioned by the European Parliament—co-authored by prominent researchers from the Trans European Policy Studies Association (TEPSA) and the Romanian Institute for the Study of the Asia-Pacific (RISAP)—concluded definitively that China's increasingly assertive, aggressive foreign policy, combined with its massive state-subsidized industrial overcapacity, renders unmitigated European market exposure an entirely unacceptable, existential security risk. This landmark study intellectually validated the European Commission's urgent implementation of robust, unilateral economic defenses as the only logical course of action (European Parliament, "EU-China relations: De-risking or de-coupling", 2024).<sup>53</sup> Furthermore, think tanks such as the Clingendael Institute observe that while the term "de-risking" serves beautifully as a unified, politically palatable mantra within the corridors of Brussels, its actual, on-the-ground implementation varies dramatically among sovereign member states; for instance, Spain deliberately adopts a highly narrow definition aimed almost exclusively at mitigating specific vulnerabilities in green-tech supply chains to avoid broader protectionism, while certain Eastern European actors, particularly Hungary, maintain a highly skeptical, permissive stance toward continued Chinese capital integration (Clingendael Institute, "National perspectives on Europe's de-risking", 2024).<sup>37</sup>

Conversely, deeply entrenched Chinese academic interpretations and official government missives consistently characterize the European Union’s highly touted "de-risking" doctrine as mere semantic sophistry designed to mask old-fashioned, illegal protectionism. Authoritative reports issued by China's Ministry of Commerce, heavily amplified by state media apparatuses, routinely label the EU's Economic Security Strategy, the Net-Zero Industry Act, and the sweeping CRM Act as thinly veiled forms of "de-Chinaization." Chinese analysts argue these policies are heavily dictated by intense geopolitical pressure emanating from the United States rather than genuine European economic necessity (Global Times, "China's response to EU Economic Security Strategy", 2024).<sup>55</sup> Furthermore, prominent Chinese scholars argue vehemently that Europe's attempt to frame China's hard-won manufacturing competitiveness, advanced economies of scale, and natural geographic advantages in critical mineral extraction as a form of "economic coercion" is logically and empirically flawed. They warn that utilizing this flawed logic to justify massive, punitive tariffs actively risks disrupting essential international supply chains and could ultimately derail global climate goals by artificially inflating the cost of the green transition for European consumers (Global Times, "China's response to EU Economic Security Strategy", 2024; Observer Research Foundation, "China's response to de-risking strategy", 2024).<sup>55</sup>


### 5. Policy relevance for the thesis

This intense examination of contemporary challenges serves as the analytical climax of the undergraduate thesis, seamlessly connecting the dry historical treaties and rigid institutional frameworks discussed earlier directly to the immediate, high-stakes, practical challenges defining the 2024-2025 macroeconomic era. By meticulously defining the strict statutory parameters of the Critical Raw Materials Act and carefully detailing the explosive economic fallout of the battery electric vehicle anti-subsidy investigations, the thesis can brilliantly highlight the excruciating, almost impossible delicate balance the European Union must continuously maintain. Europe is desperately attempting to protect and rebuild its domestic industrial manufacturing base—via instruments like the Net-Zero Industry Act—without inadvertently derailing its own ambitious, legally binding climate transition goals, which currently rely entirely on the unfettered importation of affordable, highly advanced Chinese clean technology. Furthermore, integrating the grim, pragmatic business insights harvested from the EU Chamber of Commerce provides the thesis with crucial, irrefutable empirical evidence proving that high-level geopolitical friction and political risk are no longer theoretical concepts, but are directly, measurably altering corporate capital allocation strategies and permanently reshaping the reality of doing business in China.

### 7. Gaps or claims requiring further verification

The most significant, glaring gap in the current academic literature and institutional reporting concerns the ultimate, real-world market enforcement and long-term macroeconomic impact of the highly complex "price undertakings" negotiated between the European Commission and Chinese electric vehicle exporters in early 2025. Because these minimum import price agreements are legally convoluted and are only just entering their initial phases of active implementation, their actual effectiveness in genuinely neutralizing massive Chinese state subsidies without blatantly violating foundational WTO non-discrimination principles remains entirely unproven and demands rigorous future empirical study. Additionally, the sheer industrial and logistical feasibility of the European Union successfully achieving the Critical Raw Material Act's incredibly demanding 40% domestic processing benchmark by the looming deadline of 2030 is heavily, and rightly, questioned by prominent metallurgical industry experts, given the continent's current, severe lack of advanced refining infrastructure, prohibitive energy costs, and notoriously slow environmental permitting bottlenecks.
