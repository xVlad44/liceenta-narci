Evolution of the strategic partnership
This section corresponds to thesis subsection 1.1, “Evolution of the EU-China strategic partnership,
1975-2025.”
Short research summary
EU-China relations began as a limited diplomatic and trade relationship and evolved into a dense but
increasingly conflictual partnership. Official EU sources identify 1975 as the year diplomatic ties were
established, while the 25th EU-China summit in July 2025 explicitly marked the 50th anniversary of those
relations. The first formal trade pillar came through the 1978 Trade Agreement between the EEC and
China, which created a Joint Committee for Trade, while the 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation
Agreement broadened the relationship from trade to wider economic cooperation and created a Joint
Committee structure that still underpins the legal framework. 
The relationship did not develop linearly. The 1989 Tiananmen crackdown triggered a political freeze and
the EU arms embargo on China, which EU institutions still trace to the Madrid European Council
declaration of 27 June 1989. Although trade continued, security cooperation remained structurally limited
thereafter . China’s WTO accession on 11 December 2001 marked a new phase of economic deepening and
accelerated China’s integration into the multilateral trade order , which in turn intensified EU expectations
regarding reciprocity, subsidy discipline, and market access.

The relationship was politically upgraded in 2003 to a comprehensive strategic partnership, and the EU-
China 2020 Strategic Agenda for Cooperation, adopted in 2013, became the highest-level joint guidance
document for the partnership. However , official EU language shifted markedly in 2019, when the
Commission and the High Representative described China simultaneously as a cooperation partner, a
negotiating partner, an economic competitor, and a systemic rival. That document is the clearest
official marker of the EU’s move away from a predominantly engagement-based framework toward a more
differentiated and defensive one. 
The 2020-2023 phase combined ambitious economic negotiation with severe political deterioration. In 
September-November 2020, the EU and China concluded and approved the Geographical Indications
Agreement, the first significant bilateral trade agreement between them. In December 2020, the two sides
also reached an agreement in principle on the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment, but the
investment file stalled after the 2021 sanctions-and-countersanctions cycle and the European Parliament’s
decision that CAI consideration was “justifiably frozen.” Russia’s war against Ukraine, China’s alignment
choices, and widening concerns over overcapacity, subsidies, export controls, and coercion accelerated the
policy turn. 
Since 2023, the central EU formula has been “de-risking, not decoupling.” The June 2023 European
Council conclusions stated that the EU would reduce critical dependencies and vulnerabilities and “de-risk
and diversify where necessary and appropriate,” while affirming that the EU “does not intend to decouple.”
The December 2023 summit reiterated that de-risking means strengthening resilience by addressing
critical dependencies in specific sectors, and the July 2025 summit linked the 50th anniversary to a more
explicit debate over “critically unbalanced” trade, overcapacity, market access, and Chinese restrictions on
rare earths and permanent magnets. 
Key verified facts
Diplomatic relations between the European Communities/EU and China date to 1975, and the 25th
EU-China summit held on 24 July 2025 was officially framed as the 50th anniversary of those ties.
(EEAS, 2013; Consilium, 2025) 
The 1978 Trade Agreement established an EEC-China Joint Committee for Trade and provided that
trade in goods and services would be carried out at market-related prices and rates. (EUR-Lex, 1978,
Article 7/Article 9 verified through EUR-Lex search excerpt) 
The 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement broadened the relationship and created a 
Joint Committee to monitor the agreement, review cooperation schemes, and examine obstacles to
trade and economic cooperation. (EUR-Lex, 1985, Article 15 verified through EUR-Lex search excerpt) 
The EU arms embargo on China was established in the wake of Tiananmen in a European Council
declaration of 27 June 1989; that legal-political legacy still shapes security cooperation. (European
Parliament answer on behalf of the Commission, 2023) 
China became a WTO Member on 11 December 2001 under its Protocol of Accession. (WTO, 2001) 
The relationship was upgraded to a comprehensive strategic partnership in 2003, and the EU-
China 2020 Strategic Agenda for Cooperation was jointly adopted in 2013. (Springer article
abstract/intro, 2025; EEAS, 2013)

Main academic interpretations
Academic literature converges on the idea that the relationship combines continuity in economic
interdependence with change in political trust and security framing. Jing Men argues that continuity
persists where material interdependence, institutionalisation, and shared functional interests remain
strong, whereas change is driven by normative divergence, strategic competition, and external shocks.
(Men, 2025) 
Richard Maher’s classic argument remains relevant: the 2003 strategic partnership raised expectations
that EU-China relations would become “comprehensive,” but the relationship did not move decisively
beyond trade into coherent alignment on wider political and security objectives. (Maher , 2016, abstract as
checked) 
Frédéric Krumbein’s 2025 article interprets the last decade as a move from liberal/institutionalist
engagement toward a more realist EU approach focused on reciprocity, economic security, and risk
management. In his reading, 2019 was the decisive watershed because it formally introduced the
competitor/rival language that still structures EU policy. (Krumbein, 2025) 
Policy relevance for the thesis
For the thesis, the core analytical point is that EU-China economic relations did not move from cooperation
to conflict in a simple sequence. Instead, economic ties deepened even while the political interpretation of
those ties changed. That distinction is essential for later chapters dealing with trade, competition, and
policy instruments: the framework shifted from engagement under conditions of growing
interdependence to engagement under conditions of managed risk. The historical periodisation should
therefore foreground both continuity of exchange and the progressive hardening of EU policy language and
instruments.

Short research summary
The legal backbone of EU-China economic relations remains relatively narrow compared with the scale of
bilateral trade. The enduring base is still the 1985 Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement, which
formalised trade and broader economic cooperation and established a standing Joint Committee. Over
time, this treaty framework was complemented by a dense network of summitry, sectoral dialogues, WTO
disciplines, and a limited number of specialised bilateral agreements, but not by a comprehensive free trade
agreement. The EU-China 2020 Strategic Agenda for Cooperation became the highest-level joint roadmap
from 2013, while the annual summit and the High-Level Economic and Trade Dialogue emerged as the
main institutional venues for economic coordination. DG Trade currently identifies the HLED as the principal
platform in which the Commission Executive Vice-President and the Chinese Vice Premier address major
trade and investment issues. 
In legal terms, the bilateral framework is nested inside the WTO system. China’s 2001 WTO accession
deepened trade and investment relations, but it also strengthened the EU’s expectation that China would
move toward non-discrimination, subsidy discipline, transparency, and commercially meaningful market
access. These expectations remain central to the EU position in recent Commission and Council documents.
Yet the WTO layer has also been conflictual: China requested consultations against the EU in DS516 in 
December 2016 over the Union’s price-comparison methodology in anti-dumping proceedings involving
Chinese products, and a panel was established in April 2017. That dispute captures the long-running
controversy over China’s market economy status and the EU’s continued use of alternative methodologies
where significant distortions are found. 
The only major sector-specific bilateral agreement that was fully concluded and approved in the last decade
is the EU-China Agreement on Geographical Indications. Official Council materials describe it as the first
significant bilateral trade agreement signed between the EU and China. It protects 100 EU agri-food
GIs in China and 100 Chinese GIs in the EU, and provides for an extension covering 175 additional GI
names on each side four years after entry into force. The agreement was signed on 14 September 2020
and the EU adopted the conclusion decision on 23 November 2020. 
By contrast, the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment remains the clearest case of institutional non-
completion. Negotiations formally started in 2014, followed years of exchanges on market access and level
playing field rules, and concluded in principle on 30 December 2020. Commission materials emphasise
that the CAI contained commitments on market access, state-owned enterprises, transparency of subsidies,
prohibition of forced technology transfer , and sustainable development. However , the agreement was never
ratified. The European Parliament stated in May 2021 that consideration of the CAI was “justifiably frozen”
until Chinese counter-sanctions on EU actors were lifted. 
Since 2019, the institutional framework has been increasingly supplemented by trade-defence and
economic-security instruments. These include the FDI Screening Regulation (2019), the Dual-Use
Regulation (2021), the Anti-Coercion Instrument (2023), and the European Economic Security Strategy
(2023), which explicitly links trade, investment, export controls, technology leakage, coercion, and supply-
chain resilience. The 2023 strategy groups risks into four broad categories: supply-chain resilience, critical
infrastructure security, technology security and leakage, and weaponisation of dependencies/
economic coercion. It also links this risk framework to instruments such as trade defence, the Foreign
Subsidies Regulation, FDI screening, and potential outbound-investment scrutiny. Together , these measures

Main academic interpretations
Academic work on institutionalisation stresses that the EU-China relationship has become deeply routinised
even when political trust is weak. Fanoulis and Song argue that a great deal of the literature on EU-China
relations is best understood through the institutionalisation of cooperation, rather than through a simple
binary of success or failure. (Fanoulis & Song, 2021, abstract checked) 
On the CAI, policy-legal commentary is divided. A CEPS legal-policy paper argued that the agreement largely
delivered on the EU negotiating mandate in terms of market-access and level-playing-field objectives, while
other European institutional analyses have emphasised its political fragility and the institutional difficulty of
ratification after the 2021 sanctions dispute. The core scholarly implication is that institutional law could
no longer be insulated from broader geopolitical conflict. (CEPS, 2021; European Parliament material,
2021) 
On the market-economy-status dispute, legal scholarship visible in the materials checked suggests that the
expiry of paragraph 15(d) of China’s WTO Accession Protocol did not automatically remove the EU’s capacity
to treat Chinese prices as distorted in anti-dumping methodology. That helps explain why the legal conflict
shifted from the older “non-market economy” vocabulary toward the EU’s newer language of significant
distortions. (Manchester legal manuscript abstract; Commission 2024 distortions report)

Policy relevance for the thesis
For the thesis, this subsection should show that EU-China economic relations are governed by a hybrid
legal architecture: an old core treaty (1985), multilateral WTO commitments, limited sectoral bilateral
agreements, and a rapidly expanding layer of autonomous EU trade-defence and economic-security
instruments. That combination matters analytically because it explains why the EU can intensify
restrictions, screening, and investigations without first renegotiating its foundational bilateral treaty with
China. It also helps explain why the relationship can remain formally cooperative while becoming
substantively more defensive.

Current challenges and the shift to de-risking
This section corresponds to thesis subsection 1.3, “Current challenges: de-risking vs. de-coupling.”
Short research summary
The language of de-risking marks the EU’s central conceptual shift in its China policy. Official EU sources do
not define the term as a total reduction of economic ties; rather , they define it by contrast with decoupling.
In the June 2023 European Council conclusions, the EU committed to reducing critical dependencies and
vulnerabilities, including in supply chains, and to de-risking and diversifying “where necessary and
appropriate,” while stating explicitly that it “does not intend to decouple.” Ursula von der Leyen’s March
2023 China speech made the same point even more clearly: it is “neither viable nor in Europe’s interest” to
decouple from China, but Europe must address excessive dependencies, technology leakage, and economic
coercion. The December 2023 summit then translated this into operational language by saying that “de-
risking but not decoupling” aims to strengthen resilience by addressing critical dependencies in specific
sectors. 
The official policy driver is now economic security. The checked version of the European Economic
Security Strategy identifies four broad families of risk: supply-chain resilience, physical and cyber-
security of critical infrastructure, technology security and technology leakage, and the 
weaponisation of dependencies or economic coercion. The same strategy ties these risks to a three-part
policy approach: promote competitiveness, protect against risks, and partner internationally. This is
the most authoritative official framework currently available for explaining why the EU is moving from
generic market engagement toward targeted resilience measures.

electric vehicles, semiconductors, and data/dual-use technologies. In her March 2023 speech, von der

Main academic interpretations
The strongest recent interpretation in the checked literature is that de-risking is not shorthand for
decoupling. The European Parliament’s 2024 study defines de-risking as an early-stage policy intended to 
manage risks arising from economic and technological engagement with China, and explicitly states that
the emerging consensus is to avoid broad decoupling in favour of targeted measures where concrete
dependencies or risks exist. (European Parliament study, 2024, pp. 8 and 87 as verified) 64
14

--- PAGE 15 ---

Jing Men’s 2025 article interprets de-risking as evidence that the EU is trying to preserve core
interdependence while selectively managing vulnerability, especially in technology and security-
sensitive domains. In her framework, the persistence of climate dialogue and trade exchange alongside
tighter screening and controls is not contradictory; it is the institutional expression of a hybrid relationship.
(Men, 2025) 
Frédéric Krumbein goes further and reads the shift as a move toward a more structural realist EU posture.
In his interpretation, the EU has not abandoned engagement, but has reinterpreted it through the lenses of
power asymmetry, geopolitical rivalry, and economic security. This helps explain why the policy mix now
combines traditional trade dialogue with export controls, FDI screening, subsidy investigations, and
diversification efforts. (Krumbein, 2025) 
Policy relevance for the thesis
For the thesis, the key analytical distinction is that de-risking is a selective policy logic, not a general
theory of disengagement. It is best presented as the EU’s attempt to reconcile three realities: first, the
economic scale of the China relationship; second, the strategic risks created by concentrated dependencies;
and third, the domestic political need to show that trade openness will not be allowed to undermine
resilience, industrial policy, or security. In later empirical chapters, this distinction will be useful for
interpreting EU action on EVs, batteries, rare earths, semiconductors, and investment screening without
mischaracterising the EU as pursuing blanket decoupling.
